# Appendix G: Why the Human Brain Has Not Evolved

Appendix G — Why the Human Brain Has Not Evolved: Biological Limitations

Question:
If humanity is so intelligent—why do we still do stupid things? Why do we know that the climate is collapsing—but continue burning oil? Why do we know that war is a loss for everyone—but continue fighting?

Answer: Our brains didn’t evolve for the modern world.

Fact: The human brain has not changed in 10,000 years

Homo sapiens
became anatomically modern approximately 300,000 years ago. The last significant changes to the brain: ∼10,000 years ago (the transition to agriculture). Since then—zero evolution.

Why? Evolution works through natural selection: those better adapted survive and pass on genes. But for the last 10,000 years, humanity created culture, technology, and medicine—which reduced the pressure of natural selection. Even people with “bad genes“ (disease predisposition, low IQ) survive and reproduce. Result: the brain is stuck at the level of primitive hunter-gatherers.

How the brain worked 100,000 years ago (and still works today)

Environment:
tribe of 50--150 people; territory of ∼100 km²; planning horizon of one day to one season; threats: predators, hunger, cold.

What was needed for survival:

Short-termism
—“Food now \> food in a year.“ If you see a berry—eat it immediately (tomorrow it may be gone).

Local thinking
—“My tribe \> strangers.“ Resources are limited. Outsiders = competitors.

Fast threat response
—See a shadow in the bushes → run (better to flee 100 times incorrectly than to fail to flee from a lion once).

Hierarchy
—The alpha gets food and mates first. Submission = survival.

Ego as protection
—“I am right, others are not.“ If your tribe believes in you—you are the leader. Doubt = weakness.

This worked perfectly... 100,000 years ago.

Why the same brain DOESN’T work now

Modern world:
“tribe“ = 8 billion people; territory = the entire planet; planning horizon = 50--100 years; threats: climate, AI, nuclear war.

What is needed for survival:

Long-termism
—“Food in 50 years \> food now.“ If you clear a forest today—your grandchildren will have no oxygen.

Global thinking
—“The entire planet = one system.“ CO₂ has no borders. If China pollutes—the whole Earth suffers.

Analysis of complex risks
—Climate change is not “a shadow in the bushes“ (an obvious threat). It is statistics, models, projections—an abstract threat. The brain doesn’t process it as real.

Horizontal networks
—Hierarchy doesn’t work for global problems. Coordination among equals is needed (nations, corporations, communities).

Accepting mistakes
—“I was wrong. Let’s fix it.“ But the ego says: “If I admit a mistake—I appear weak.“

Result: We live in the world of 2025 with the brain of 100,000 BCE. This is a catastrophic mismatch.

Concrete examples: How the brain fails us

Example 1: Temporal Discounting

Experiment: a person is offered (A) $100 today, or (B) $200 in a year. Most choose A—even though B is mathematically superior (100% return). Why? The brain doesn’t trust the future. 100,000 years ago, “in a year“ might never come (you could die from illness, a predator, hunger).

How this harms today: politicians choose short-term decisions (cut taxes now), ignoring long-term consequences (debt, pension collapse); people eat fast food (pleasure now), ignoring the outcome (diabetes in 20 years); corporations maximize quarterly profit, destroying ecosystems (consequences in 50 years).

Example 2: The Availability Heuristic

The brain assesses risk based on how easily an example comes to mind. Experiment: “What is more dangerous—sharks or falling coconuts?“ Most say sharks. Reality: coconuts kill ∼150 people/year; sharks ∼5 people/year. Why the error? Because we’ve seen films about sharks. We haven’t seen films about killer coconuts.

How this harms: people fear terrorism (vivid news, photos, emotions)—but ignore air pollution (kills 1,000 times more, but it’s boring). People fear plane crashes (dramatic footage), but not driving (car accidents kill 100 times more). The brain responds to drama, not statistics.

Example 3: Confirmation Bias

The brain seeks information that confirms what we already believe. Experiment: people are shown identical climate data. Those who believe in climate change → see evidence of warming. Those who don’t → see natural fluctuations. Same fact. Two conclusions.

Why? The brain conserves energy. Changing a belief = rewriting neural pathways = energetically expensive. Easier to ignore contradictory facts. How this harms: politicians ignore data that contradicts their ideology; people don’t change their minds even when they see evidence; corporations deny harm even when their own research shows the opposite.

What to do about it? (The Anthosphere as solution)

The problem: we cannot change the brain biologically (evolution is too slow). The solution: we change the system so it compensates for the brain’s flaws.

Compensation 1: AI as an “external prefrontal cortex“

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) is the part of the brain that controls impulses and plans long-term. In most people, the PFC is weak—it can’t keep up with emotions and short-term desires. AI = an artificial PFC.

How it works: a person wants to clear a forest (short-term gain). AI says: “Stop. Let’s look at the consequences in 20 years: oxygen loss, soil degradation, 30% crop losses. Is it worth it?“ AI doesn’t block the decision. But it forces a pause and a moment of thought.

Compensation 2: Multi-contour review against the ego

The ego says: “I am right. Others are not.“ When a leader has ego plus power = catastrophe (the history of the 20th century proves this). Solution: no decision is made by one person alone. The system: architect proposes → AI verifies the mathematics → other architects verify the logic → public audit (anyone can challenge). Ego cannot break through four filters.

Compensation 3: Transparency against confirmation bias

All data is public. If an architect says “we should build a dam,“ the AI shows: benefit (+500 MW energy), loss (200 km² of flooded forest, 15 species will disappear). The data is accessible to everyone. If someone sees that the architect is hiding the losses—they can challenge through public audit. Transparency = impossibility of manipulation.

Compensation 4: Long-term metrics against temporal discounting

The system measures consequences over 20--50 years, not just now. Every decision is evaluated on two metrics: short-term (1--5 years): does this improve life now? and long-term (20--50 years): is this sustainable? If a decision is good now but bad in 20 years—AI marks it as “high-risk.“ Example: “Clear the forest = +$10M now, but -$100M in 20 years (loss of soil, water, oxygen).“ The system forces visibility of long-term consequences.

Final thought: We cannot change the brain, but we can change the system.

The human brain is short-sighted, emotional, prone to biases, full of ego. This is not “bad.“ It is simply a fact of evolution. But we can build a system that: compensates for short-sightedness (AI shows long-term consequences); filters emotions (multi-contour review); overcomes biases (data transparency); and limits ego (rotation, public audit).

The Anthosphere is not “ideal people.“ It is a system for imperfect people—one that works not despite our brain’s flaws, but by accounting for them.
