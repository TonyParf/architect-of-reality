# Appendix B: The Biology of Evil

Appendix B — The Biology of Evil: When the Brain Is Sick, Not the Person Bad

Even if we build the perfect system—what do we do with people whose brains are physically incapable of controlling aggression? This is not a philosophical question. It is a medical one.

The research:
Adrian Raine (University of Pennsylvania, USC), whose landmark work
The Anatomy of Violence: The Biological Roots of Crime
(2013) drew on 25+ years of brain scanning of violent criminals in U.S. prisons.

His conclusion: “Most violent crimes are committed by people with physical brain abnormalities.“ This is not about “bad people.“ It is about sick brains.

Key findings:

The prefrontal cortex (PFC)—the brain’s “brakes“:
Controls impulse, planning, empathy, understanding consequences. In violent criminals: the PFC is smaller or damaged (11--14% below the norm). A person with a damaged PFC physically cannot stop an impulse. A normal person feels anger → the PFC says “stop, think“ → they don’t hit. A person with a damaged PFC feels anger → immediately hits. No brakes.

The amygdala—the “fear and aggression center“:
In violent criminals, hyperactive or damaged—incorrectly interpreting signals. Someone bumping them accidentally in a crowd reads as an attack.

Serotonin:
In violent criminals, 20--40% below normal—chronic stress, inability to calm down, aggression as a defense response.

Testosterone + cortisol:
High testosterone (aggression, dominance) plus low cortisol (no fear of consequences)—the “perfect storm“: aggressive and unafraid of what follows.

Childhood trauma plus genetics:
The highest violence risk comes from genetic predisposition (damaged PFC) combined with trauma (abuse or neglect in childhood). Raine showed: genetic predisposition plus a loving upbringing → low risk. Genetic predisposition plus violence → 80%+ risk.

Other researchers confirm this. James Fallon (neuroscientist) discovered his own brain had the same anomalies as psychopaths—but he didn’t become a criminal, because he grew up in a loving environment without trauma. His conclusion: “Genes load the gun. Environment pulls the trigger.“

Robert Sapolsky (Stanford): “Free will is an illusion. Behavior is determined by biology (genes, hormones, brain structure) plus environment.“ If a person kills because of a brain tumor that damaged the PFC, we say they are sick. But if a person kills because of a genetically small PFC—why do we say they are evil rather than sick? There is no difference between a tumor and genetics. Both are biology.

The ethical shock: Does this mean “not guilty“?

Adrian Raine’s answer: No—they are still responsible. But the response is treatment and protection of society, not punishment.

Analogy: a person with epilepsy struck someone during a seizure. We don’t call them evil. But we isolate them until we find a treatment. The same logic applies to aggression: the person is isolated and treated until the brain is corrected.

How this changes the justice system:

Now:
Offender → court → prison → punishment. Logic: “They did evil because they are bad. Punishment will correct them.“ Reality: 70%+ recidivism in the U.S. Punishment doesn’t work.

Anthosphere:
Offender → AI diagnostics → MRI → identification of anomalies → treatment. Logic: “They did evil because their brain is sick. Treatment will correct them.“ Reality: under 20% recidivism (Norway, where prisons are treatment centers).

Treatment technologies that already exist or are in development include: SSRIs (raising serotonin to reduce aggression); mood stabilizers (reducing impulsivity); transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS—magnetic pulses that strengthen the PFC, reducing aggression by 30--50% after 20+ sessions); neurofeedback (learning to regulate the amygdala); cognitive-behavioral therapy (30--40% reduction in aggression); and in the future, gene therapy to correct mutations affecting serotonin or PFC structure (experimental, projected for humans by ∼2035).

How this works in the Anthosphere:

Childhood screening (ages 5--10):
AI analyzes genetics, behavior, and environment. If risk is identified—the family receives support (not stigma): a psychologist for the child, support for parents, regular check-ups. Goal: prevent aggression from developing before it becomes a problem.

Adolescence (ages 12--18):
AI monitors social signals, wearable stress data, school performance. If concern is flagged: automatic referral to a psychologist. Not punishment. Support.

Adulthood (18+):
Voluntary monitoring—wearables, annual MRI (free), AI recommendations. If an anomaly is found: an offer of treatment, not compulsion.

After a crime has occurred:
Not prison—a treatment center. MRI and blood analysis, diagnosis, treatment (medication, therapy, TMS), monitoring, gradual return to society under supervision. Goal: correct the cause, not punish the symptom.

Currently, 1.2 million people are in U.S. prisons for violent crimes. The Anthosphere trajectory—through prevention—could reduce that number by the majority within a generation.
